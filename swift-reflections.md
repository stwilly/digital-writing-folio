### What are some of the constraints and tensions that Hayles notes have motivated writers to experiment in the field of E-Lit (there are many!)

Some of the constraints and tensions Hayles mentions are the "changing nature of computers" (p. 3), "readers... expectations [of literature] formed by print"(p. 4), and the suggested "large-scale social and cultural changes... bound up with the spread of digital culture" (p. 3). Obviously the mere matter of e-lit not being possible in print by definition will encourage writers to push the bounds of the digital: _how far from print can we get? how deranged and strange a text can we make that we couldn't before?_

### What defines the ‘Three Generations’ of E-Lit that Flores refers to?
#### First Gen: 
- pre-World Wide Web, pre-formal category of e-lit,
- "characterised by... pioneering works that emerged between 1952-1995".
- As access to devices that could be used to make e-lit became more commonplace, so did e-lit practitioners.

#### Second Gen: 
- Internet access, increased circulation of e-lit
- specific tools (Flash (_aw, Flash_), Director, etc.) "empowered a generation of authors to create electronic literature, even if they didn't have a high level of programming skill..."
- the barrier to entry only becomes more accessible as the 2nd Gen progressed
- "academia has been a powerful engine for expanding this audience... leading to growth of the field"

#### Third Gen:
- "... social media networks and widely adopted platforms and apps..." = "... massive production and audiences..."
- number of practitioners increases again by orders of magnitude
- separates itself by its authors having an implicit affinity for digital platforms and the resultant e-literary experimentation.
- There is a disconnect with the prior Generations regarding Thrid Generations's e-lit literary ancestry: "disconnected from the artistc and literary traditions of the past, they were more directly formed by digital culture".
- Hayles notes, most print media now is merely the print of a formerly digital work, with less distinction between the two as distinct modes of media.

### In terms of AI and ethics, consider where your own boundaries are in terms of what is unacceptable or acceptable use of AI. What do you see as potential ethical use-cases of AI (e.g., for research, for furthering open-source knowledge, for testing new areas of learning)?

It feels foolish to be absolutely pro- or anti-AI. I do think it has the potential to be to future people similar to what the Internet is for us, in terms of a technological revolution, and changing of the status-quo creative process. It may not have what we recognise as the advantages of the Internet, but perhaps AI will improve to the point where it replaces, for instance, the multi-billion dollar Hollywood entertainment complex. _We_ (and _I_) may think that it would be tragic for these actors' jobs to be replaced (but then, how sorry should we feel for _HOLLYWOOD_ actors?), let alone the people who don't gain celebrity status (as valuable as that might be) and millions for their work: set designers, lighting riggers, grips, location scouts, costume/makeup designers, etc., etc. , etc. Would we only mourn this loss because it's familiar? I could see how some people down the line will look back at this celebrity culture and entertainment monopoly and say "good riddance".

There's also this feeling of complicity when I use AI. I need to do some research to find the most ethical corporation out there (oxymoron), but then, I have an Instagram account, a Facebook account, I buy clothes from shitty retailers, so many of the ways I enjoy living where I do is predicated on the exploitation of _others_. Is this different? It may be a drop in the ocean, but the ocean is made of drops, and it is another drop.

#### Author; Narrator; Narratee; Reader:
Unless the narrattee can be understood as the reader, in which case perhaps it conforms more to the Classical narrative model, _Untitled Docuent_ seems to conform to what Aaresth identifies as a Modernist, Author-Narrator/Narratee-Reader communication model. The monologic unspooling, remembering, recontextualising, and reframing doesn't reach an intended listener, with the author acknowledging their role as narrator of a story which they tell between distracting visits to their Google Drive, which unearth memories which influence later drafts of the story told within the fiction. We're simulating being in an author's head while they navigate their creative process, iterating on a work as they are influenced in the narrator's reality.
