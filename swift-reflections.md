### What makes a work a piece of e-literature?
A piece of e-literature is reliant on the digital space to function correctly or present fully. While some works of e-lit could be more feasibly adapted to physical/print media, if a digital work can be transcribed without adaptation to physical media, then it's not e-lit. I suppose that answer mainly focuses on the "e-", but the literature part feels more self-explanatory: writing-based.

_Tarako Gorge_ was cute but kind of merely novel to me. Great idea, well executed, but not very arresting, personally. The writing was my favourite aspect of _The Gathering Cloud_. ### What are some of the constraints and tensions that Hayles notes have motivated writers to experiment in the field of E-Lit (there are many!)

Some of the constraints and tensions Hayles mentions are the "changing nature of computers" (p. 3), "readers... expectations [of literature] formed by print"(p. 4), and the suggested "large-scale social and cultural changes... bound up with the spread of digital culture" (p. 3). Obviously the mere matter of e-lit not being possible in print by definition will encourage writers to push the bounds of the digital: _how far from print can we get? how deranged and strange a text can we make that we couldn't before?_

### What defines the ‘Three Generations’ of E-Lit that Flores refers to?
#### First Gen: 
- pre-World Wide Web, pre-formal category of e-lit,
- "characterised by... pioneering works that emerged between 1952-1995".
- As access to devices that could be used to make e-lit became more commonplace, so did e-lit practitioners.

#### Second Gen: 
- Internet access, increased circulation of e-lit
- specific tools (Flash (_aw, Flash_), Director, etc.) "empowered a generation of authors to create electronic literature, even if they didn't have a high level of programming skill..."
- the barrier to entry only becomes more accessible as the 2nd Gen progressed
- "academia has been a powerful engine for expanding this audience... leading to growth of the field"

#### Third Gen:
- "... social media networks and widely adopted platforms and apps..." = "... massive production and audiences..."
- number of practitioners increases again by orders of magnitude
- separates itself by its authors having an implicit affinity for digital platforms and the resultant e-literary experimentation.
- There is a disconnect with the prior Generations regarding Thrid Generations's e-lit literary ancestry: "disconnected from the artistc and literary traditions of the past, they were more directly formed by digital culture".
- Hayles notes, most print media now is merely the print of a formerly digital work, with less distinction between the two as distinct modes of media.

But then she lauds Shelley Jackson's _SNOW_ (rightfully so) because of 'how the author interacts with the platform, and she's an e-lit author of renown and prestige, and she's only using it as a "printing press" so it isn't even _really_ Instapoetry'... yeah... ## Thursday
### In terms of AI and ethics, consider where your own boundaries are in terms of what is unacceptable or acceptable use of AI. What do you see as potential ethical use-cases of AI (e.g., for research, for furthering open-source knowledge, for testing new areas of learning)?

It feels very tricky, give me ten years and I might have a better stance. It feels foolish to be absolutely pro- or anti-AI. I do think it has the potential to be to future people similar to what the printing press is for us, in terms of a technological revolution, and changing of the status-quo creative process. It may not have what we recognise as the advantages of the printing press (literacy, access to/dissemination of information, etc.), but perhaps AI will improve to the point where it replaces, for instance, the multi-billion dollar Hollywood entertainment complex. _We_ (and _I_) may think that it would be tragic for these actors' jobs to be replaced (but then, how sorry should we feel for _HOLLYWOOD_ actors?), let alone the people who don't gain celebrity status and millions for their work: set designers, lighting riggers, grips, location scouts, costume/makeup designers, etc., etc. , etc. Would we only mourn this loss because its familiar? Hard to say. But I could see how some people, the next generation, down the line, will look back at this celebrity culture and entertainment monopoly and say "good riddance". I'm slowly convincing myself of it now, a bit.

When it comes to the replacement of creative endeavour or emplyment, I think AI is just highlighting the coercive, softly homicidal nature of capitalism. Why should artists need to sell their work for money, and by extension, for shelter, for food: to live, basically. Why should anyone? It isn't 1950, it isn't 1800. How much are the structures of capitalism actually inevitable and unoverthrowable?

There's also this feeling of complicity when I use AI. I need to do some research to find the most ethical corporation out there (oxymoron), but then, I have an Instagram account, a Facebook account, I buy clothes from shitty retailers, so many of the ways I enjoy living where I do is predicated on the exploitation of _others_. Is this different? It may be a drop in the ocean, but the ocean is made of drops, and it is another drop.

I wonder if the next big cultural shift might be a recession to older forms, rather than an ambracing of AI. The AI-first school feels very, step into the bright future, progress is a straight line kind of thing. It feels uncertain as to whether that's the direction everything is moving. Then again, maybe rather than regressing (or not regressing, re-embracing?) older forms, maybe people won't entrench deeper into the modes and ways of recent history and will be inspired to embrace something totally new. But then again-again, AI is hardly a representation of anything new in the scheme of our capital-digital paradigm.

Begrudging tolerance. There's an important aspect to that relationship where I am aware that I don't own anything digitally, all of my digital interactions are conducted through these mega-corporate templates, but no action has happened. Of course, if everyone did, then the power floods from the hands of its corporate owners, but... people don't want to be eaten.

### Discuss Jenny's second GenAI/'JenAI' blog post and then reflect upon the importance of tracking tools of making (e.g., for iterative purposes, for troubleshooting, as research capture situated in a particular time (since tech evolves so quickly), to network/pool knowledge with peers, to situate ideas in relation to the field).

A large benefit of working with paper and pens, simple word processors, is that there's not so much about the tools themselves that needs to be recorded, project to project. When it comes to the digital landscape, this becomes more important, between updating/changing technology, difficulty in resuming work on a project that perhaps has a higher bar to entry by nature of its complexity/inaccessibility skill-wise, the recording of a process and therefore the tools used becomes vital. 
Especially when it comes to the digital writing space, where innovation feels so much the focus of the form, sharing experimental successes and failures with other practitioners serves the field, so that innovation may beget more innovation, failure might beget success.
